## PROMPT A — Deep Research (Perplexity / ChatGPT)

```
DEEP RESEARCH PROMPT — HORMUZ STRAIT CLOSURE: WHO BENEFITS AND WHY
Intent: Invest — Tactical (6–24 months)

You are acting as a senior research analyst. The Strait of Hormuz has been
effectively closed to Western-aligned shipping since 4 March 2026, following
US-Israeli strikes on Iran under Operation Epic Fury. Tanker traffic is down
approximately 70%. Oil is at $95–100 WTI. The IEA has released 400 million
barrels from strategic reserves.

Conduct comprehensive, unbiased research on who benefits from this disruption
and why. Do not filter by sector or asset class — identify every credible
beneficiary across the full economy and capital markets.

1. MACRO BENEFICIARIES
   Which countries, economies, and sectors gain a structural or cyclical
   advantage from the Hormuz closure? Quantify where possible.
   Consider: energy producers outside the Gulf, commodity exporters,
   alternative trade route economies, currency beneficiaries, and any other
   macro-level winners.

2. SECTOR AND INDUSTRY BENEFICIARIES
   Map every sector that benefits — directly or indirectly — from sustained
   Gulf supply disruption. For each:
   - What is the transmission mechanism (how does the disruption translate
     into revenue, pricing power, or margin expansion)?
   - Is the benefit cyclical (reverses on resolution) or structural (persists
     regardless of how quickly Hormuz reopens)?
   - What is the magnitude and likely duration?

3. LISTED EQUITY BENEFICIARIES
   Identify the most compelling listed equities globally across all sectors.
   For each name:
   - Why does it benefit specifically?
   - What has its stock done since 28 February 2026?
   - What is the analyst consensus view?
   - What are the key risks to the thesis for that specific name?

4. HISTORICAL ANALOGUES
   Has a comparable supply shock played out before?
   Consider: 1973 OPEC embargo, 1979 Iranian revolution, 2022 Russia-Ukraine
   energy crisis, 2019 Abqaiq attacks.
   What happened across sectors — not just energy — in each case?
   Which beneficiary theses held for 6+ months? Which reversed quickly?

5. SECOND AND THIRD-ORDER EFFECTS
   Beyond the obvious energy price beneficiaries, what less-discussed
   consequences create investable opportunities?
   Consider: food security, industrial supply chains, shipping route economics,
   currency flows, defence procurement, energy transition policy, commodity
   substitution, regional power shifts.

6. WHAT CONSENSUS IS MISSING
   Where is the market currently under-pricing a beneficiary?
   Where is it over-pricing one?
   What is the most contrarian credible thesis?

7. CATALYST CALENDAR
   Upcoming dates, events, policy decisions, or earnings prints that could
   move individual beneficiary theses. Be specific.

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Bottom line first: 3–5 sentences identifying the highest-conviction
  beneficiaries and the strongest transmission mechanisms
- Section-by-section findings with source citations
- Distinguish clearly: (a) confirmed facts, (b) analyst consensus,
  (c) emerging signals, (d) your own inference
- 15–25 high-quality sources minimum
- Flag data gaps and areas of genuine uncertainty explicitly
- Do not avoid sectors that may be politically sensitive — assess all of them
```

## PROMPT B — AlphaSense

```
ALPHASENSE PROMPT — HORMUZ STRAIT CLOSURE: WHO BENEFITS AND WHY
Intent: Invest — Tactical (6–24 months)

You are conducting investment-grade research on the beneficiaries of the
2026 Strait of Hormuz closure. The Strait has been effectively closed to
Western-aligned vessels since 4 March 2026. Focus on primary source
intelligence: earnings calls, broker research, expert transcripts, and
company filings.

1. FUNDAMENTAL VALIDITY
   Which companies and sectors are showing genuine fundamental improvement
   — in pricing power, volume, or margin — as a direct result of Gulf
   supply disruption?
   What are management teams saying on earnings calls and at investor
   conferences about the impact on their business?
   Is corporate behaviour (capex, M&A, hiring, production guidance) consistent
   with the beneficiary thesis?

2. BROKER AND ANALYST ACTIVITY
   Where is broker research concentrating? Which sectors and names are
   receiving upgrades, target price increases, or initiations of coverage
   since 28 February 2026?
   Are there significant divergences in analyst views — if so, what is
   driving the disagreement?

3. KEY PLAYERS AND COMPETITIVE DYNAMICS
   For the top beneficiary sectors identified: who are the main listed
   companies? What do market share trends and competitive positioning look
   like? Where are advantages durable versus cyclical?

4. REGULATORY AND POLICY TAILWINDS
   What government actions, policy announcements, or procurement decisions
   have been triggered by the Hormuz crisis that create durable tailwinds
   for specific sectors or companies?

5. BEAR CASES
   For each major beneficiary sector: what would invalidate the thesis?
   What are the most credible sceptical views in the research?
   What are practitioners flagging as risks that the market appears to be
   dismissing?

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Findings structured by section
- Source every significant claim (company name, date, document type)
- Distinguish confirmed facts from analyst consensus from emerging signals
- Flag explicitly where evidence is thin, conflicting, or absent
- Include bear cases with equal rigour to bull cases
```

   Which sectors started hot and are cooling? Which are building later?

   Which accounts are driving the beneficiary conversation?
   Are they credible practitioners (fund managers, sector analysts, operators)

5. CROWDING SIGNALS

   Which still look contrarian or under-owned based on the conversation?
   Is there evidence that certain names or themes are being pushed

6. RED FLAGS AND BLIND SPOTS
   What negative signals or dissenting views are present but being dismissed?
   What are serious practitioners flagging as risks the broad narrative ignores?
   Which beneficiary thesis looks most fragile on closer examination?

OUTPUT FORMAT:
- Sentiment summary (2–3 sentences) covering the overall narrative landscape
- Section findings with representative examples and named sources where available
- Clear distinction between signal and noise
- Flag explicitly if any part of the narrative appears promotional or coordinated
- Do not filter by sector — cover all beneficiary conversations
