## SLIDE 10 — Breakeven Economics: Who Survives $60 Oil

Create a content slide with the CAPE top-bar layout.

Title: **Breakeven Economics — Who Survives $60 Oil**
Subtitle: *Lifting costs and fiscal breakevens tell very different stories · The spread between them is the producer vulnerability gap*

Layout: text on the left (~40%), two images stacked on the right (~60%).

Left panel:
**Two different numbers**
Lifting cost (cash cost to produce one barrel) ranges from $3–5/bbl in Middle East onshore to $40–60/bbl in deepwater and Arctic. Fiscal breakeven — the price needed to balance a national budget — is almost always much higher.

**Why the spread matters**
A country can produce profitably at $20/bbl but need $90/bbl to fund its government. That gap drives political behaviour inside OPEC — quota cheating, emergency production hikes, geopolitical adventurism.

**Regional cost hierarchy**
Middle East onshore: $3–8/bbl lifting cost. North American shale (Permian): ~$15–25/bbl full cycle. Offshore West Africa: $25–35/bbl. Deepwater Gulf of Mexico: $35–50/bbl. Arctic: $50–80/bbl.

**Full-cycle discipline**
US shale companies now publish breakeven figures but these often exclude land costs, hedging losses, and debt service. True full-cycle breakeven is 15–20% higher than stated.

US shale will continue growing, but at a more measured pace (~400–600 kb/d/year) than the 1.0–1.5 mb/d/year of 2015–2019. The unconstrained production surge that capped prices last decade is unlikely to repeat.

Subtitle: *Road transport electrification addresses only ~57% of oil demand · Shipping, aviation, and petrochemicals are structurally non-electrifiable near-term*

Layout: text on the left (~45%), image on the right (~55%).

Left panel:
**The demand stack**
Light vehicles (cars/LDVs): ~26% of oil demand — where EVs are making inroads. Heavy transport (trucks, ships, planes): ~31% — no commercially scalable zero-carbon alternative at current costs. Non-combusted uses (petrochemicals, plastics, fertilisers): ~14% and growing — not burned, cannot be replaced by electricity.

**The EV narrative conflation**
The dominant media narrative conflates the electrification of passenger cars with the decarbonisation of the entire oil demand stack. These are very different problems on very different timelines.

**The EV paradox**
EVs eliminate gasoline demand but introduce new oil-derived demand: more synthetic rubber per tyre (heavier vehicles), more carbon fibre, more electronics-grade chemicals. The oil demand reduction from one EV sold is partially offset by the extra petrochemical demand created.

**What actually declines**
Gasoline demand is the most vulnerable: ~22% of total oil demand. Diesel and aviation are slower to electrify. The petrochemical complex grows. The net demand reduction from the EV transition by 2030 is ~3–5 mb/d — not 20+.

## SLIDE 17 — Geopolitics: The Hormuz Chokepoint

Title: **Geopolitics — The Hormuz Chokepoint**
Subtitle: *20% of global oil through a 3-mile navigable channel · Zero credible alternative routing at scale · The permanent option premium*

Layout: text on the left (~40%), map image on the right (~60%).

Left panel:
**The numbers**
14–16 mb/d of crude oil plus 5–6 mb/d of refined products transit the Strait of Hormuz daily. At $75/bbl, approximately $1bn of oil value per hour. The strait narrows to a 3-mile navigable channel at its tightest point.

**The alternative routes — and their limits**
Saudi Arabia's East-West pipeline (Petroline): ~5 mb/d capacity. UAE's Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline: ~1.5 mb/d. Together, they can reroute ~40% of Hormuz throughput at most — and both require significant lead time to activate.

**Why the threat is structural, not binary**
Iran has never closed the Strait but has repeatedly demonstrated capacity to harass tankers, plant limpet mines, and disrupt traffic. A partial closure — drone swarms, targeted strikes, mining — doesn't need to stop traffic entirely to move prices $20–40/bbl. The threat premium is permanent.

**The six-producer concentration**
Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Iraq, Iran, and Qatar collectively produce ~25–28% of global supply — all dependent on Hormuz access.

**The rerouting**

**The shadow fleet**

**The dual scenario**

**Where the value comes from**
Traders do not own crude. Value is extracted from: price dislocations between markets (arbitrage), freight and logistics advantages, information asymmetry, and term contract relationships with NOCs. None of these advantages are permanent.

**2021–2022: an exceptional period**
Ukraine-driven dislocation created arbitrage opportunities that inflated trading margins 5–10x normal levels. This is now normalising. The structural margin for commodity trading in equilibrium markets is thin.

**Why traders are not the right investment vehicle for the oil thesis**
Trading margin compression is structural: more market participants, satellite imagery and AIS vessel tracking compress information advantages, price transparency is increasing. The value in commodity trading accrues to partners and management, not to listed equity holders.

4. **Non-OPEC supply overshoot** — Guyana + Brazil + US production growth simultaneously exceeds demand growth by 2+ mb/d in 2026.
5. **Sustained global recession** — reduces demand by 1–2 mb/d, combined with supply overhang creates 5–6 mb/d of surplus.
